White Nights ~ Dostoyevski

So, I just finished reading White Nights, and honestly, I wasn’t sure what to expect. I knew it was one of Dostoevsky’s shorter works, and I’ve always heard that his stuff is pretty intense. But this one—while still a little heavy—felt different. It’s almost like it’s about the quiet kind of heartbreak, not the dramatic, over-the-top stuff, but that slow, aching kind of loneliness. The story is about this guy (who we never actually get a name for, by the way) who’s living in St. Petersburg and is super isolated. He’s one of those people who spends all their time in their head, dreaming of things that probably will never happen. I get it, though, because he’s kind of like the person who feels everything, but also doesn’t really know how to connect with anyone. Then he meets Nastenka, a girl who is, like, going through her own dramatic love life, and he falls for her almost instantly. I really loved how Dostoevsky captured the feeling of waiting for something to happen, that almost desperate feeling of longing when you’re not sure what you’re waiting for. And the whole white nights thing—where the sun barely sets in St. Petersburg—works so well with the whole vibe of the story. The nights feel endless, like the narrator’s emotions, which are stuck in this loop of dreaming about what could be, even when reality keeps telling him otherwise. It’s beautiful, but also so sad. What hit me the most was how real the emotions felt. The narrator’s inner world was super relatable—like, he’s this hopeless romantic who has built up this perfect version of Nastenka in his mind, even though she’s not really available to him. And it made me think about how we can sometimes get lost in our own dreams or our idealized versions of people, instead of seeing things clearly. It’s messy, but that’s what makes it feel so human. I’ll admit, there were times I got frustrated with the narrator, especially when he kept holding onto a fantasy that was never going to happen, but I guess that’s kind of the point. It shows how love can be so confusing and painful, and how we can sometimes trap ourselves in our own emotions. At the same time, though, it made me reflect on the way we all deal with loneliness, and how hard it is to break out of that cycle. In the end, White Nights is one of those stories that stays with you because it’s so emotionally raw. It’s not just about a guy falling in love with a girl—it’s about the messiness of feelings, how you can want something so badly but not be able to make it real. I didn’t expect it to hit me as much as it did, but now, even though it’s a short read, I feel like I’ve learned something about myself and the way people connect with each other. Verdict: I would totally recommend White Nights to anyone who likes character-driven stories about love, loneliness, and the complexities of the human heart. It’s a short read but packs a punch. If you like deep, introspective stories, this one’s for you. It might leave you feeling a little heavy but in a good way, you know?

Vesa Skeraj
